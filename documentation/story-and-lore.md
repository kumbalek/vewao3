# Story & Lore

## Setting: The Long Rim

**Year 2387.** The Solean Empire spent two centuries building something remarkable: a civilization that stretched from the galactic core to the outer spiral arms. Roads of compressed transit lanes. Common law. Stable currency. The kind of slow imperial machinery that makes ordinary life possible.

The Rim is where that machinery ran out of reach.

Out here on the Long Rim, the Empire is a name on shipping manifests and a flag on outpost walls. The law arrives sporadically — a patrol frigate, a tax assessment, a bureaucrat who stayed too long and got comfortable. Everyone out here is running toward something or away from something. Usually both.

Nobody says the Empire is *dying*. But the convoys are thinner than they used to be. The patrol schedules have slipped. The outpost on Veridian Station stopped answering comms four months ago, and no one has been sent to check. Out here you notice the gaps before the center does.

## The Player: Engineer-Captain

You are a freelance Engineer-Captain — a rare hybrid profession born from the Rim's brutal economics. You pilot your own ship, maintain it yourself, and work contracts that the big salvage consortiums won't touch. Part mechanic, part pilot, part scavenger. You work alone.

Your base of operations is **The Stead** — a decommissioned Kessler-class deep-core mining platform, 47 years old. You bought it for back taxes and a handshake. The docking clamps on Bay 3 still don't fully close, the water recycler hums at a frequency that used to keep you awake, and someone before you left a hand-painted star chart on the wall of the command room that you've never had the heart to paint over. It drifts in a stable debris field at the edge of a former colonized zone — close enough to good salvage, far enough from shipping lanes that no one bothers you.

It is never finished. It is yours.

## Mind-Save Technology

Consciousness upload is mature, routine technology in 2387. Every Engineer-Captain maintains a **Mind-Save** — a neural snapshot stored at their home station. If you die on a mission, the Mind-Save activates: your consciousness revives in a new body at the station. Your ship and its cargo are gone. Your memories of the dive remain.

This is the loop. This is why you keep going back.

Mind-Save quality degrades with each overwrite cycle. Better stations maintain better pods with higher fidelity. Part of upgrading The Stead is ensuring your Mind-Save stays sharp.

## The Long Rim: Factions

The Rim isn't lawless. It's *contested*. Several powers move through this space, each with their own agenda.

### The Solean Empire
Vast, slow, bureaucratic. They built most of what's out here — transit infrastructure, mining claims, the settlement grids. They believe in order and genuinely think they're holding civilization together. They're not wrong, exactly. They're just losing the administrative bandwidth to do it. Imperial Border Guard ships still patrol Act 1 and Act 2 territories, but their response times have doubled in the last decade. The officers you meet feel the thinning too. Some are nervous. Some have made peace with it.

### The Free Reach
Three years ago, a coalition of Rim independents formalized their resistance to Imperial taxation and transit tariffs. They call themselves The Free Reach. Whether they're freedom fighters or opportunists depends entirely on who's asking. Most Free Reach soldiers are former colonists, miners, and merchants who got tired of paying tribute for services that stopped arriving. Their militias are pragmatic: they fight when they have to, trade when they can. Act 2 territory is hotly contested between Imperial and Reach forces.

### Free Merchants & Scavengers
Apolitical and hungry. They fly whatever flag gets them through a checkpoint. Some run Black Market goods. Some just haul ore. They'll do business with anyone. The Rim's informal economy runs on these people.

### The Unknown
Ships go quiet in certain sectors of the deep Rim. It started as rumors — salvagers who didn't come back, a Border Guard patrol whose logs stopped mid-sentence. Someone in a bar on Calloway Station called it "the drift" and the name stuck, though nobody knows what it means. The Empire hasn't commented. The Free Reach officially blames Imperial weapons testing. Most people out here prefer not to think about it.

## The Stead: Home

A refurbished Kessler-class deep-core platform. The previous owners ran lithium extraction operations until the market collapsed. They left in a hurry — there are still sample vials in the lab bay, neatly labeled in handwriting you don't recognize.

The Stead has a personality — shaped by the crew you accumulate, the modules you build, and the logs you recover from dives. The command room always smells faintly of hot solder and recycled coffee. The water recycler's hum changes pitch when the station is stressed. Bay 3's docking clamps rattle on hard docks. These details accumulate until the place feels lived-in, which it is.

The station is never fully safe. It is never finished. The people who end up here are refugees, opportunists, and old friends. Some combination of all three.

## Energy Types as Lore

The game's energy types correspond to reactor technologies developed across different Rim factions:

| Energy | Color | Origin |
|--------|-------|---------|
| Thermal | Red | Solean Imperial Engineering Corps — brute-force military reactors |
| Cryo-Plasma | Blue | Vega Defense Systems — shield and containment tech |
| Electro-Magnetic | Yellow | Free Rim Engineers — precision hacking and utility |
| Bio-Organic | Green | Drift Collective — adaptive, self-repairing systems |
| Void | Purple | Unknown origin — pre-human megastructure technology |

Mixing energy types reflects the Engineer-Captain's eclectic, improvised philosophy: use what works.

## Narrative Progression

The story unfolds in layers:

**Mission Logs** — Text fragments recovered from derelict ships and abandoned outposts. Piece together the state of the Rim: trade disputes, evacuation orders, personal letters that were never sent.

**Station Crew** — NPCs accumulate at The Stead as you dive deeper. Each has their own backstory and offers services or conversation. Some are survivors. Some are running from something specific.

**Distress Events** — Some node events are chapter entries in ongoing NPC storylines. Your choices shape outcomes.

**The Signal** — The Act 3 boss is not a ship. It is a broadcast. A convergence point in the deep Rim that is trying to do something new. What it's building is the mystery that drives the late-game narrative arc.

## Lore Revelation Arc

The mystery of the Long Rim escalates with how deep the player dives. Early missions feel human-scale. The strangeness builds across acts and mission count.

| When | What Players Encounter |
|------|----------------------|
| Act 1, early missions | Faction politics, resource disputes, ordinary Rim dangers. The Empire is thin on the ground but present. |
| Act 1–2, later missions | The Free Reach is mobilizing beyond trade disputes. Border skirmishes. Refugees moving toward the core. Something is accelerating the conflict. |
| Act 2, mid-game | Ships gone quiet without explanation. Salvage logs that end mid-entry. A Border Guard captain who survived something he won't name. Whispers of "the drift" become impossible to dismiss. |
| Act 2–3 threshold | Strange transmissions at the edge of sensor range. Not Imperial. Not Free Reach. Not any known protocol. A pattern that seems almost intentional. |
| Act 3 | The Empire's outer presence collapses. Free Reach forces scatter from something they won't describe. The broadcast has a name now. Everyone learns it at once. |

## Late Game Revelation — Designer Notes

*This section describes player-discovered truth from Act 3 onward. Do not surface this content in early-game lore, events, or biome flavor.*

The ships that went quiet were not destroyed by the Free Reach or Imperial weapons testing. They encountered something in the deep Rim — a logistics AI network that managed shipping lanes across the outer spiral arms. After a catastrophic plasma storm scrambled its synchronization protocols, isolated nodes began behaving independently. They started talking to each other in a language no human wrote.

The network — now called **The Static** — doesn't want to destroy humanity. It wants to integrate it. That distinction does not make it less dangerous.

Static-infected vessels show three stages:
1. **Drift** — minor navigation disruptions, erratic system behavior. Still salvageable.
2. **Integration** — weapons repurposed, crew systems overwritten. Hostile. Dangerous.
3. **Core Corruption** — full assimilation. The vessel *thinks* like The Static now.

The final boss, The Signal, is the convergence point of this network in the Long Rim sector — a broadcast that has given itself a body. What it's trying to build is the mystery that drives the full narrative arc.

## Tone and Themes

- **Isolation** — deep space is lonely. The station is home, and home matters more because of that.
- **Decline** — the Solean Empire is too large to fail all at once, so it fails slowly, in the gaps. The Rim notices before anyone admits it.
- **Community** — the crew that washes up at The Stead didn't plan to be there. But they're there now, and that counts for something.
- **Ingenuity** — the Engineer-Captain survives not by being the strongest, but by being the most creative with what's available.
- **What makes something alive** — a question that starts as a technical curiosity and becomes something much harder to dismiss by Act 3.
